Part 01

Who Am I?

Introduction & Self-Assessment

Growing up, I was always the person taking things apart — literally and figuratively — to understand how they worked. I would rather spend an evening figuring out why a system was inefficient than simply accept that it was. That instinct to analyse, question, and build logical frameworks has shaped who I am today: someone who is quiet but deeply observant, more interested in getting things right than getting credit for them.

I tend to process information internally before speaking, prefer working through problems independently, and am drawn to abstract systems and underlying logic rather than surface-level appearances. I am not naturally the loudest voice in a group discussion, but when I do contribute, it is usually because I have spotted an inconsistency or a more efficient way of approaching the problem. I also recognise that this can come across as detached at times, and one area I am actively working on is communicating my reasoning more clearly so that others can follow my thought process, not just the conclusion.

My personal values, explored through the values card activity in CPP, consistently ranked accuracy, independence, and competence at the top. I believe that good decisions are built on good information, and that taking the time to verify and understand a problem properly is rarely wasted effort, even when it feels slower in the short term.

A Time I Failed and Learned

In secondary school, I was part of a team project analysing survey data for a class assignment. Confident in my own ability to work things out alone, I built the entire data model myself without checking my assumptions with my teammates, and submitted it the night before the deadline. It turned out I had misread one of the survey questions, which skewed several of our conclusions, and the whole group's grade suffered as a result.

On reflection, I realised that my preference for working independently had blinded me to a simple step: asking someone else to sanity-check my logic before finalising it. In a later project, I deliberately built in a peer-review checkpoint before any submission. The following project not only avoided the same mistake but was flagged by our teacher as one of the more rigorous pieces of analysis submitted that term. That experience taught me that independent thinking is valuable, but it is strongest when paired with deliberate verification from others.

Proudest Achievement

My proudest achievement was building a simple data-tracking spreadsheet system for my secondary school's recycling programme, which allowed the environmental club to monitor contamination rates across different bin locations over time. What started as a personal curiosity project — I wanted to know whether better signage actually changed behaviour — became the tool the club used to justify a redesign of bin labelling across the whole school.

The data showed a measurable drop in contamination after the redesign, which gave the club concrete evidence to present to the school administration for further funding. This reflects the NP values of Responsibility (following through on a self-initiated project to completion) and Integrity (presenting the data honestly, including the parts that did not support my initial assumptions).

Greatest Talent

My greatest talent is structured problem-solving — the ability to break down a complex or messy situation into its component parts, identify the root cause, and build a clear, logical process to address it. I have developed this skill through consistent practice: working through case studies, building spreadsheet models for personal projects, and spending time in online courses on logic and data analysis simply because I found them interesting. This is a talent I have actively cultivated because I find genuine satisfaction in the process of figuring things out properly.

Part 02

Why Am I Here?

Purpose & Beliefs

Why I Am Pursuing the Common Business Programme

I chose the Common Business Programme (CBP) because I was not yet ready to commit to a single specialisation. I had broad interests spanning finance, data analytics, operations, and international trade — and CBP felt like the most intellectually honest choice. It allows me to explore the underlying logic of different business functions before deciding where my analytical strengths would be best applied.

More importantly, I am here because I want to understand how organisations make decisions using data and structured reasoning rather than instinct alone. I am drawn to roles where precision and evidence matter — where a well-built model or a correctly interpreted dataset can change the outcome of a business decision.

Something Meaningful to Me

What matters most to me is the idea of getting things right through careful, evidence-based reasoning rather than assumption. This came into focus during my secondary school data-tracking project, when I realised that a well-built system could replace guesswork with verifiable fact. I find this kind of structured, investigative thinking deeply meaningful, and it is what draws me toward business roles centred on data, analysis, and process improvement.

My Beliefs, Purpose & Five-to-Ten Year Vision

I believe that good decisions are built on accurate information and rigorous logic, not persuasion alone. My purpose is to build a career where my analytical strengths are put to direct use — solving concrete business problems with data, structure, and evidence. I also believe in continuous, independent learning; much of what I know was learned outside the classroom, simply because I was curious enough to keep digging.

In five to ten years, I see myself working in a role centred on business analysis, operations, or financial data — perhaps as a Business/Data Analyst, an Operations & Supply Chain Analyst, or an Internal Audit/Risk Analyst. I want to be someone whose conclusions are trusted because the underlying analysis is sound, not because I am the most persuasive voice in the room.

Why Ethics Matter to My Life

Ethics matter to me because data and analysis can easily be manipulated to support a predetermined conclusion, and I want no part of that. I have seen examples of selective data presentation used to mislead, and I find that kind of intellectual dishonesty deeply uncomfortable. Ethical behaviour, to me, means presenting findings accurately even when the results are inconvenient, and resisting the temptation to shape evidence around a desired outcome rather than letting the evidence speak for itself.

Part 03

How?

Three Preferred Jobs

As a CBP student, I have researched three job roles that align with my INTP personality and my Realistic, Conventional, and Investigative interests — all of which point toward analytical, structured, evidence-based work rather than people-facing persuasion roles.

Job 01
Business / Data Analyst
Corporate · Financial Services · Technology · Consulting
Roles & Responsibilities
Collects, cleans, and interprets business data to support decision-making. Builds dashboards and reports, identifies trends and anomalies, conducts root-cause analysis, and presents findings to stakeholders in a clear, evidence-based manner. Often works closely with finance, operations, or product teams to translate raw data into actionable recommendations.
Qualifications & Experience
Diploma or degree in Business Studies, Business Analytics, or a related field. Proficiency in SQL, Excel, and visualisation tools such as Power BI or Tableau. Certifications such as the Google Data Analytics Certificate or Microsoft Power BI Data Analyst Associate strengthen a candidate's profile.
Skills & Attributes
Strong logical and analytical reasoning, attention to detail, comfort working independently with large datasets, proficiency in spreadsheet modelling and basic SQL, and the ability to communicate technical findings to non-technical stakeholders.
Why I Chose This
A direct match for my Investigative and Conventional interests — it rewards careful, structured analysis over persuasion or sales ability. After attending a Career Fair session where a Business Analyst described spending her day building models to test hypotheses before presenting conclusions, I recognised the exact kind of work I find satisfying: methodical, evidence-driven, and independent.
Job 02
Operations & Supply Chain Analyst
Logistics · Manufacturing · Retail · Trading
Roles & Responsibilities
Monitors inventory levels, analyses procurement and logistics data, identifies inefficiencies in supply chain processes, forecasts demand using historical data, and recommends process improvements to reduce cost and delay. Highly systems-oriented, often involving direct interaction with physical operations such as warehouses or production lines.
Qualifications & Experience
Diploma in International Trade & Business, Business Studies, or Supply Chain Management. Familiarity with ERP systems (e.g., SAP), Excel-based forecasting models, and basic statistics is beneficial. Internship experience in warehousing, procurement, or logistics coordination is an advantage.
Skills & Attributes
Strong process orientation, comfort with structured procedures and systems, numerical and analytical skills for forecasting and inventory optimisation, and a practical, hands-on approach to solving operational problems.
Why I Chose This
Aligns closely with my Realistic interest — tangible, concrete problems rather than abstract strategy discussions. A conversation with an industry mentor at a logistics networking session reinforced this: the best operations analysts sit with messy real-world data and find the practical inefficiency hiding inside it — precisely the investigative, hands-on problem-solving I enjoy.
Job 03
Internal Audit / Risk Analyst
Banking & Finance · Professional Services · Corporate Risk Management
Roles & Responsibilities
Evaluates an organisation's financial controls, processes, and compliance with regulations. Conducts control testing, identifies process weaknesses or fraud risk, prepares audit reports with clear documentation, and recommends corrective actions. Demands meticulous attention to procedure, accuracy, and objective, unbiased reporting of findings.
Qualifications & Experience
Diploma in Accountancy, Banking & Finance, or Business Studies. Progression toward certifications such as ACCA, CIA (Certified Internal Auditor), or CFA is common. Internship experience in audit firms or corporate finance departments strengthens employability.
Skills & Attributes
High attention to detail, strong adherence to procedure and documentation standards, objective and independent judgement, comfort working through structured checklists and frameworks, and the ability to present findings factually without bias.
Why I Chose This
A strong fit for my Conventional interest and INTP preference for objective, logic-based evaluation. At a Banking & Finance industry webinar, an internal auditor described her job as being paid to question whether things are actually as they appear on paper — matching my own instinct to verify rather than assume.

On Gender and KSAOs

Gender, as a personal characteristic, does not inherently determine whether an individual possesses the Knowledge, Skills, Abilities, and Other characteristics required for any of the three roles above. The KSAOs that matter for a Business/Data Analyst, an Operations & Supply Chain Analyst, or an Internal Audit/Risk Analyst — analytical rigour, attention to detail, structured reasoning, objectivity — are developed through education, experience, and effort, not determined by gender. While historical data may show gender imbalances in certain industries, these reflect structural and societal patterns rather than inherent capability gaps. I believe in evaluating individuals on their demonstrated competencies, and I would apply that same standard to my own career development.

Courses, CCAs & Continuing Education

Part 4a — Top Three Course Choices

I have researched three diplomas offered by the School of Business and Accountancy and evaluated them based on the career pathways they open, the curriculum content, and how well they align with my analytical interests.

1
First Choice — BF
Diploma in Banking & Finance
My first choice because of its strong foundation in financial markets, investment analysis, risk management, and financial planning — all directly aligned with my aspiration to work in Internal Audit or financial data analysis. The curriculum's emphasis on how capital flows, how risk is quantified, and how financial institutions operate appeals to my Conventional and Investigative interests, where precision and evidence-based reasoning are non-negotiable. The BF diploma also provides a natural stepping stone toward professional qualifications such as ACCA and, in the longer term, the CFA. Graduates typically enter banking operations, risk management, financial advisory, and compliance functions.
2
Second Choice — ITB
Diploma in International Trade & Business
My second choice for its focus on supply chain, logistics, and trade operations — directly relevant to the Operations & Supply Chain Analyst role. Modules covering logistics management, trade documentation, and global supply chains would give me practical, process-oriented knowledge that matches my Realistic interest in tangible, hands-on systems. Understanding how goods and capital move across borders also adds a useful dimension to financial risk analysis, as supply chain disruptions are an increasingly significant component of operational risk.
3
Third Choice — BS
Diploma in Business Studies
My third choice for its broad coverage of Business Analytics, Operations Management, and Data-Driven Decision Making. This diploma would directly support a Business/Data Analyst career, equipping me with both the quantitative tools and the wider business context needed to interpret data meaningfully. Its flexibility also keeps multiple career pathways open, which is valuable at this stage when I am still refining my specialisation preference.

Part 4c — Continuing Education & Lifelong Learning

I am aware that a polytechnic diploma is a strong foundation, but not the endpoint of my education. I have identified a post-graduation university pathway I am actively working towards.

National University of Singapore — NUS Business School
Bachelor of Business Administration (BBA)

NUS Business School's BBA is a four-year undergraduate programme with strong analytical rigour and a well-regarded Business Analytics specialisation track, alongside options in Finance and Operations Management. For polytechnic applicants, admission is assessed holistically, taking into account academic results (GPA), relevant CCA involvement, an aptitude-based interview, and in some cases a written assessment.

I am drawn to NUS because of its emphasis on quantitative and analytical coursework, its strong industry linkages with banks and consulting firms in Singapore's financial district, and the credibility the NUS name carries with employers in data-intensive roles. During the NUS information session at the Career Fair, the admissions representative noted that polytechnic applicants who clearly demonstrate sustained, self-directed engagement with analytical or quantitative work — rather than just academic results — tend to stand out in the interview process, reinforcing the importance of the data-tracking and analytics projects I have already begun building.

This pathway reflects my belief that learning does not end with graduation. The pace of change in data analytics and financial regulation means that my knowledge base must be continuously refreshed, and I am committed to treating education as a lifelong, self-directed practice rather than a box to tick.
